# 4. [pac]: peace

* pacific
+ [adj]: making or tending to make peace; appeasing; conciliatory.
+ [adj]: of a peaceful nature or disposition; not warlike; mild; tranquil; calm.
- the Pacific Ocean
- pacific ↔ bellicose

* pacify
+ [v]
- to pacify the critics
- to pacify the rebels

* pacifist
+ [n]: a person who believes that war and violence are unjustifiable.
- "I want you to make love, not war. I know you've heard it before."

* pacifier
+ [n]
- A pacifier can calm a crying baby down.

* pact
+ [n]
- A formal agreement between two individuals or parties.
- non-aggression pact
- make a pact

* appease
+ [v]
- They attempted to appease international opposition.

* pace ['peɪs] (from Latin)
+ [prep]: politely disagree or contrast with someone's view, no offense; with respect; notwithstanding.0
≈ excuse me, pardon me
- The evidence suggests..., pace Professor Jones, that...

# 5. [crim]: crime

* crime
+ [n]: a very serious mistake; a huge regret.
- It would be a crime to travel (all the way) to Australia and not stop in Sydney.

* criminal
+ [n]: a person who has committed a crime.
- you are a criminal
+ [adj]: relating to crime
- they are charged with conspiracy to commit criminal damage.

* criminology
+ [n]: the interdisciplinary study of crime and deviant behavior.

* decriminalize
+ [v]: stop treating (something) as illegal or as a criminal offense.
- Do you think we should decriminalize alcohol?
- The question of whether prostitution should be decriminalized.

* criminalize
+ [v]: turn an activity or someone into a criminal offense by making it illegal.
- His view is that the state should not criminalize drug use but discourage it.
- These punitive measures would further criminalize travelers for their way of life.

* incriminate
+ [v]: to charge with or show evidence or proof of involvement in a crime or fault, accuse.
+ [v]: to cause to appear guilty of or responsible for something.
- from "in crime"
- He claimed that the drugs had been planted to incriminate him.

* incriminating
+ [adj]: making someone appear guilty of a crime or wrongdoing.
- incriminating evidence

* recriminate
+ [v]

* recrimination
+ [n]
- The bitter arguments and recriminations have finally ended the relationship.

# 7. [grav]: heavy, serious, weight

* gravity
+ [n]: the force that attracts a body towards the center of the earth, or towards any other physical body having mass.
+ [n]: seriousness, severity, extreme importance
- The gravity of their crime.

* gravitation
+ [n]: the force of attraction that bodies exert on one another as a result of their mass.

* grave
+ [n]: used as an allusive term for death.
+ [adj]: giving cause for alarm; serious.
+ [adj]: serious or solemn in manner or appearance.
- Something is very grave.

* gravitas
+ [n]: solemn dignity
- The gravitas you might expect of a Nobel prize winner.

* gravitate
+ [v]: move towards or be attracted to a person or something.
- gravitate to something = be attracted to/by something
- Moths gravitate to a flame.
- You naturally gravitate toward people with shared values.
- Young people gravitate to their role models.

* aggravate
+ [v]: make something grave.
- Stress and lack of sleep can aggravate this situation.
- My lower back problem is aggravated.

* gravid
+ [adj]: pregnant

* grieve
+ [v]: feel intense sorrow.
- This injury grieves me.

* raven
+ [n]: a large heavily built crow with mainly black plumage.
+ [v]: hunt voraciously for prey; devour voraciously.

# 8. [lev]: light, rise

* elevator
+ [n]: lifter

* elevation
+ [n]: height above a given level, especially sea level.
+ [n]: the action or fact of raising or being raised to a higher or more important level, state, or position.
≈ promotion

* levitation
+ [n]: the action of rising or causing something to rise and hover in the air.

* levity
+ [adj]: lack of respect or seriousness when you are dealing with something serious.
- levity ↔ grave

* alleviate
+ [v]: make something levity.
- alleviate ↔ aggravate
- A great deal can be done to alleviate back pain.
- Several measures were taken to alleviate the problem.

* lever
+ [n]: a rigid bar resting on a pivot, used to move a heavy or firmly fixed load with one end when pressure is applied to the other.
- Archimedes, "Give me a place to stand, and a lever long enough, and I will move the world."
+ [v]: lift or move with a lever.
- She levered the lid off the pot with a screwdriver.

* Leverage
+ [n]: Influence
- His position as mayor gives him leverage to get things done.

* cantilever
+ [n]: a rigid structural element that extends horizontally and is unsupported at one end.
- In the gym, the cantilever is often used for pullups.

* levy
+ [n]: an act of levying a tax, fee, or fine.
- levy is (of a government or organization) to demand an amount of money, such as a tax, from a person or organization.
- A government or organization levies a tax.

* relieve
+ [v]:
- Drugs can relieve much of the pain.
